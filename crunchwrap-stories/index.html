GLM-5

The Stranger's Gift

The year was 1047. In a small holding near the River Severn, a man named Wulfstan bent his back over a strip of unplowed field. He was a gebūr—a lesser serf—bound to the land of his lord, tasked with three days of labor each week and whatever else was demanded of him. The earth was cold, his belly was empty, and his life was a gray thread of toil stretching unbroken to the grave.

Then the stranger appeared.

He stepped from nothing—no doorway, no shadow, simply was where a moment before there had been air. He wore odd garments of bright colors and strange weave, and in his hands he bore two objects of a nature Wulfstan could not name.

The stranger spoke in a tongue that was not Englisc, yet was. The words were wrong, clipped and flat.

"Hey, buddy. Here." He held out something wrapped in paper of unnatural smoothness. "Crunchwrap Supreme. And this—" He pressed a cup of strange green liquid into Wulfstan's frozen hands. "—is Baja Blast. You're welcome."

The stranger stepped backward and vanished into the same nothing from which he had come. He had been there for perhaps four heartbeats.

Wulfstan stared at the objects.

"Hwæt is þis?"
What is this?

He sniffed the wrapped thing. An aroma unlike anything he had ever known—rich, spiced, almost meaty, yet carrying hints of something sweet and strange. His hands trembled. It had been two days since his last real meal.

"Se fremda... hē forlēas mē."
The stranger... he left me this.

He did not understand the paper. It was too smooth for vellum, too strong for common leaf. But the food inside—

He bit.

The flavors exploded. Meat and cheese and something crisp, a sauce that burned with a fire both sweet and terrible. He had never tasted anything like it. Salt and fat and spices from lands beyond his knowing. He ate with the desperation of a man who has known hunger as a constant companion.

Then he lifted the cup. The liquid inside was the color of a summer sky, cold as well-water, and when he drank—

"God almihtig! Hwæt is þis drync?"
God almighty! What is this drink?

It fizzed on his tongue. Sweet beyond honey, with a taste he could not name—like lime, but not quite; like herbs, but nothing the village wise-woman had ever gathered. He drained the cup and crushed it in his fist, marveling at how the strange material crumpled.

He looked around, half-expecting the stranger to return and demand payment. But the field was empty save for the crows and the cold wind off the river.

The Aftermath

That evening, Wulfstan told no one. Who would believe him? A stranger from thin air, bearing gifts of food beyond imagination? He buried the paper and the crushed cup deep in the midden heap, where no one would find them.

But he could not forget.

The taste haunted him. For the rest of his life, he sought it in every meal—every pottage, every loaf, every scrap of meat. Nothing ever matched it. The bread was too plain. The ale too bitter. The cheese too sharp and lacking that strange, impossible creaminess.

He began to ask questions of traveling merchants, of priests who had been to distant towns, of anyone who might know of spices from foreign lands.

"Hæfst þū ǣfre gehīred ymbe... sweete sause? Ymbe wyrt þe smaċað swā sumor?"
Have you ever heard of... sweet sauce? Of herbs that taste like summer?

They looked at him as if he were mad.

But his questions led him to learn. He learned of the spice trade, of far-off lands where pepper and cinnamon grew. He learned that there were tastes beyond the muddy monotony of a serf's existence. He learned that the world was larger than his lord's fields.

The Impact

Wulfstan never rose above his station. He died a serf, as he had been born. But he was not the same man he would have been.

He taught his children to wonder. He told them, on dark winter nights, that somewhere in the world there were flavors they could not imagine—colors of drink like the summer sky, wrapped foods that held meat and cheese and strange sweet fire all at once.

"Sēo woruld is brād," he told them.
The world is wide.
"Wē bēoð on hyre bedd, ac wē magon locian up."
We are in her bed, but we can look up.

His youngest son, inspired by his father's strange tales, asked too many questions and eventually found his way to a monastery—a place where curiosity was not a sin. That son became a scribe. He copied manuscripts and learned Latin and traveled to Canterbury and saw the sea.

His grandson became a merchant's factor. He handled pepper from the East and learned to haggle in the trading ports of Normandy.

His great-great-granddaughter married a freedman who had earned his way out of serfdom.

And on it went—a thread of possibility traced back to a single impossible afternoon, to a stranger who had stepped from nothing and left behind the taste of a world eight centuries away.

Epilogue

The Crunchwrap Supreme, of course, was digested. It became part of Wulfstan's body, fueling his muscles through another season of plowing. The Baja Blast passed through him like any drink.

But its memory never left. And in the end, that was the true gift—not the food, but the hunger it created. The hunger for more. The hunger for better.

A time traveler had given an eleventh-century serf a fast-food meal. And in doing so, he had given him a dream.

That dream changed a family.
That family changed everything.

Þis is sēo sǣg. Hēo is sōþ. — This is the story. It is true.
Þǣrh ān fremde giefu, manig līf wǣron āwend. — Through one strange gift, many lives were changed.

The Stranger's Gift

A Tale of Wulfric the Serf and the Blue Drink

Wulfric Æthelricson knelt in the mud of his lord's field, his calloused hands pulling weeds from between rows of barley. The year was 1065, and the summer sun beat mercilessly upon his bent back. He was forty winters old, though he looked sixty, and owned nothing but the threadbare tunic on his back and the small garden patch his wife tended behind their wattle-and-daub hut.

A sound like thunder—but from no sky—crackled through the air.

Wulfric fell backward into the mud, clutching his chest. Before him, where empty field had been, stood a... thing. Shaped like a man, but dressed in cloth so smooth and colors so bright they hurt the eyes. Blue like the sky itself, with strange markings upon the chest.

The stranger held two objects, wrapped in materials Wulfric had never seen—shiny and crinkling like dry leaves, but impossibly smooth.

"Hey dude," the stranger said, in a tongue Wulfric somehow understood, though the words were shaped strangely. "You look like you could use this. Catch!"

A soft, warm bundle sailed through the air. Wulfric caught it instinctively. It was heavy, wrapped in some kind of paper that crinkled when touched. The stranger then tossed a cup that sloshed with an unnatural blue liquid.

"That's a Crunchwrap Supreme and a Baja Blast. You're gonna love 'em. Anyway, I'm running late—gotta bounce!"

The air crackled again. The stranger pressed something on his wrist and vanished.

Wulfric sat alone in the field, holding the mysterious objects, certain that God had tested him or the Devil had tempted him. He looked around. No one had seen. The other serfs worked in distant fields.

With trembling hands, he unwrapped the strange bundle.

"Hwæt is þis? Hit is swa hefig, swa hat. Hwa wæs se wer? Com he of heofenum? Of helle? His gegyrelu wæron swa beorhte, swa strange. Ne geseah ic næfre nanwuht swylces."
What is this? It is so heavy, so hot. Who was that man? Did he come from heaven? From hell? His garments were so bright, so strange. I never saw anything like it.

He stared at the cup. The liquid inside was the color of a summer sky—impossible, unnatural. Nothing in God's creation was that shade of blue.

"Seo lyft is blæwen. Blæwer þonne ænig wæter ic geseah. Is þis drync deofles? Oþþe is þis drync engla? Swa beorht, swa stænċeð stranglice. Eala, ic eom yrreþed."
The liquid is blue. Bluer than any water I saw. Is this drink of the devil? Or is this drink of angels? So bright, it smells strongly. Alas, I am afraid.

But hunger was stronger than fear. Wulfric had eaten nothing but pottage and rough bread for months. The wrapped object smelled divine—meat and cheese and spices that made his stomach cry out.

He bit into the Crunchwrap Supreme.

The explosion of flavors nearly knocked him unconscious. The crunch of strange crispy triangles. The smooth, rich cheese—nothing like the hard cheese his lord sometimes gave him. The meat, seasoned with spices from kingdoms he'd never heard of. The soft flatbread, warm and yielding.

Tears streamed down his weathered face.

"Eadig ic eom! Eadig! Þeos mete is þæs heofonlican rices. Næfre ic ne smeaċode nanwuht swa gōd. Swylc swētnes, swylc spiciness. Min hlāford self ne eteþ swylc mete!"
Blessed I am! Blessed! This food is of the heavenly kingdom. Never have I tasted anything so good. Such sweetness, such spiciness. My lord himself does not eat such food!

He drank the Baja Blast.

The carbonation shocked him—fizzing and burning in his mouth like living fire, yet sweet and citrusy and cold despite the summer heat. He coughed, then drank more. It tasted like nothing in nature, like the essence of joy itself captured in liquid form.

"Þes drync is wundor! He is cald, swaþeahþe seo sunne þryþ. Hū mæg þis bēon? Hwa þurhfōr? For hwilcum feohsceattum?"
This drink is a miracle! It is cold, although the sun burns. How can this be? Who passed through? For what payment?

He sat for an hour, savoring every bite, every sip. When the Crunchwrap was gone and the cup empty, he wept—not from sadness, but from having experienced perfection in a life of endless toil.

Then he buried the evidence. The paper, the cup, the wrapper—all went into a deep hole beneath the barley. If his lord found out, he'd be accused of theft or witchcraft. No one would believe the truth.

The Impact

He became a prophet of sorts. In hushed tones, during the long winter nights in the communal hall, he told the other serfs of the blue-clad angel who brought food from heaven. "There is another world," he would say, "where men wear the sky itself and eat such food as would make the Archbishop weep with envy." The others thought him touched by God or mad—perhaps both—but they listened, hungry for any story that wasn't about turnips or the weather.

He lost his fear of death. Having tasted perfection, Wulfric no longer feared his inevitable end. "What awaits beyond," he told his wife, "must be full of such Crunchwraps and blue drinks. How could heaven offer less?" He worked his lord's fields with a strange serenity, humming tunes no one recognized, borrowed from the stranger's final words.

He named his next son "Baha," though he didn't know why. The word simply felt important. The boy grew up strong and clever, eventually becoming a freeman—a rarity for a serf's son—through skills as a metalworker. Some whispered the blue drink had entered Wulfric's blood and blessed his lineage.

He kept the secret of the wrapper. For fifty years, until his death in 1115 at the impossible age of ninety, Wulfric would occasionally dig up a small corner of that field. The paper had long since rotted, but he would press his hand to the soil and remember. His grandchildren thought it a quirk of old age. Only his son Baha understood.

On his deathbed, surrounded by children and grandchildren, Wulfric smiled. The priest had given last rites. The end was near.

"Ne forhtian ge. Ic fare to þæm lande þær Crunchwrapas growaþ on treowum and seo lyft is eall blæwen. Se engel hæfde gecyþed me. Ic beo þær eft gesund."
Do not fear. I travel to the land where Crunchwraps grow on trees and the liquid is all blue. The angel had shown me. I shall be there again, whole.

He died with a smile, still tasting ghost flavors of seasoned beef and lime-citrus soda on his tongue.

In the village churchyard, they carved his stone simply:

WULFRIC ÆTHELRICSON
BORN A SERF
DIED A BELIEVER

The time traveler, watching from a temporal observation deck four centuries later, read the epitaph and shrugged.

"Cool," he said, opening another Baja Blast. "Cool."

Then he checked his schedule. He was due in 14th-century France next. Some peasant was about to discover the Doritos Locos Taco.

— The End —
